2 Complete the sentences with an adjective from the box.

clear· dull· mad· sly· tough
1. There’s a fine line between being called eccentric or as as a box of frogs!
2. The book was as as ditch water; I’ve never read anything so tedious.
3. Luis won’t be upset at this setback – he’s as as old boots.
4. No one can fix the printer – the instruction manual is as as mud.
5. She’s as as a fox in business negotiations largely due to her ability to read people’s weaknesses.

Correct: 0 / 10

get on (well) · got on like a house on fire

When two people get on, their relationship is friendly; like a house on fire stresses instant rapport and shared tastes.

watch someone like a hawk

Observe very closely so nothing risky goes unnoticed — typical when looking after children or monitoring mistakes.

stick out like a sore thumb

Look obviously different or out of place compared with everything around — buildings, clothes, behaviour.

fight like cat and dog

Argue or clash all the time — fixed pairs (brothers, flatmates) often appear with this simile.

sleep like a log

Sleep very deeply and soundly, especially after exhaustion.

as mad as a box of frogs

Informal and humorous for ‘eccentric / crazy in an odd way’; near-synonym territory with eccentric in the sentence frame.

as dull as ditch water

Extremely boring — stronger than a bit dull; often collocates with books, talks, meetings.

as tough as old boots

Emotionally resilient: setbacks don’t easily upset the person — informal praise of stamina.

as clear as mud

Irony: not clear at all — instructions or explanations are confusing despite sounding helpful.

as sly as a fox

Clever and discreet when pursuing an advantage — negotiating, politics, subtle manipulation.
