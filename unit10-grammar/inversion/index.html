10  Inversion

In formal or literary English, inversion swaps subject and auxiliary (or adds do / does / did when there is no auxiliary) to heighten drama or emphasis.

1  Inversion for emphasis

After negative / restrictive fronted phrases

Typical starters include:

at no time, barely, hardly, scarcely, rarely, hardly ever, never, in no way, little, on no account, never again, no sooner, not since, not until, not only, only later, only then, only when, under no circumstances …

Hardly ever will you find this type of deciduous tree growing at this altitude.

Fronted clause — inversion in the main clause

After Not until, Only when, Only after, Only once, Not since, the inverted verb phrase belongs to the main clause that follows.

Not since we were children have we spent an evening playing board games.

These openers may also be followed by a noun phrase before the inverted verb:

Not since last spring has a stranger been seen here.

Not only … but also — and dramatic never / nor

Never must they uncover how we knew where to look for the files; nor must they ever gain access to the records.

Never again + prediction

Never again will our paths cross at such an auspicious point in the history of this universe.

Wishes with may

May next year bring all the joys you could hope for!

As, than, so (often literary)

So far had they walked through the sweltering jungle that Kristina had lost all track of time.

Authors of fiction tend to command higher advances than do nonfiction authors.

2  Inversion in conditional clauses

Inversion replaces if in formal emphasis (verb before subject).

Were it not for + noun / noun phrase

Were it not for his persuasive manner, we certainly wouldn’t have attended the event.

Past perfect opening

Had the hero known what fate held in store, he surely would have told Ada he loved her.

Were + subject + to + infinitive (unlikely futures)

Were they to take up the fight, they’d clearly need many more resources.

Should + subject (very formal)

Should you fail to complete the quest, there will be consequences.

Should you not complete the quest, there will be consequences.

3  Inversion with adverbs of time, place and movement

Put an adverbial of place / time / movement first; then use be or a verb of movement. The subject comes after the whole verb phrase. No do / does / did.

Inside the chest should have been the golden amulet.

First was a sumptuous soufflé, then came the prawns.

Here and there as place adverbs

Here comes the rain, we’d better head indoors.

There stood my parents with huge smiles on their faces.

No inversion if the subject is a pronoun.
Noun subject (inversion): There goes the bottle, washing out to sea.
Pronoun subject (standard order): There it goes, washing out to sea.
