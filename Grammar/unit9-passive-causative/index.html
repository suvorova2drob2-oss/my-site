Textbook reference (Unit 9)

Structures and examples from Passive and causative structures. In the task cards, use your own contexts (building site, retrofit, office) with the same patterns.

1. Perfect passive in subordinate / adverbial clauses

Background: -ing form of the perfect passive. Having been trained at a top university, Nina managed to find a good job… Absolute construction (background): The kitchen having been cleaned and the clothes put away, it was now time to relax. After a preposition: They went… in spite of not having been invited by the mayor’s office.

2. Several passives in a row

Two or more passive actions; the auxiliary is not repeated before each participle: The photos have been framed and hung on the wall.

3. Causative: arrange for someone to do something

have + object + infinitive WITHOUT to We should have the decorators paint the ceilings. get + object + infinitive WITH to Let’s get the children to tidy their rooms.

4. Causative: “experience” for the subject (have only)

The driving school hasn’t had anybody fail their test.

5. Promise / result: get or have + object + past participle

I’ll have your complaint reviewed thoroughly…

6. Imperative

Have / get + object + past participle Have that report written and on my desk by noon.

7. Undergo / be affected (get + past participle)

Often: caught, involved, left, stuck, trapped We… got caught up in the congestion through the town centre.

How to run the session

  • Read the reference first; then use windows 1–5 in order or pick topics.
  • Phase A, then B for each type. Treat on-screen examples as models — say your own line.
  • Feedback: perfect passive (-ing) shape, do not repeat have been unnecessarily in a passive chain, get + to vs bare infinitive after have.
